What Counts as Trauma: Military Sexual Trauma, Combat, and the Gendered Limits of PTSD

A look at who the PTSD diagnosis was built for, whose injuries get counted, and what "coming home" looks like for women who served.

By Alexander Langone • 11 min read

The first time I saw the figure I assumed there had to be some error. It was a professor casually mentioning it as if an afterthought; I wrote it down in my notebook margins along with three question marks. One third. One-third of the women going through VA screenings say yes, something sexual occurred to them during their service, something they didn't consent to. Not "in the general public." Not "during their lifetime." During their service. In uniform. I went looking for it again later, I don't always trust what I write, and it remained the same. The men's rate is approximately one in fifty. Here is the part I did not anticipate. The military is such an overwhelmingly masculine institution that when you convert those percentages into the actual number of reported incidents, men and women are closer together in terms of reporting than the headlines indicate. No group is insignificant.

That number kept running through my mind while writing my most recent essay about how long the United States military has referred to combat-related traumatic stress injuries. Soldier's Heart after the Civil War. Shell Shock after the trenches. Battle Fatigue after D-Day. PTSD after Vietnam. Each new term used for this type of injury was a battle. Each time, it was due to the refusal of a person with the injury to remain silent or allow it to become anonymous. That leads me to wonder about the soldiers whose injury is not the one displayed on the recruitment posters. What happens to these soldiers?

Judith Herman made an argument regarding this that I am having difficulty dismissing. According to her, trauma will only gain recognition within a society when a movement insists that it gains recognition. Individuals have to make that happen. She uses the PTSD diagnostic criteria as proof. The American Psychiatric Association could not include the diagnosis of PTSD until 1980, and the catalyst for its inclusion in the DSM was not one survivor community but three. They were advocating simultaneously. Vietnam veterans who would not accept "adjustment issues" as a diagnosis for their experiences after returning from Vietnam. Survivors of the Holocaust, many years after being liberated from concentration camps, were continuing to advocate for what happened to them to be recognized. Researchers and advocates focused on feminism and rape crises were chronicling the sexual abuse occurring in marital relationships and private homes. Three very distinct constituencies, side-by-side unintentionally at times, each advocating for their own segment of the definition. When the diagnostic criteria became available, each of their signatures were evident. Since then, every update has continued that same struggle over whose trauma matters and whose story gets to define the boundaries.

The military experience of women falls somewhere in between two movements advocating for the acknowledgment of various forms of trauma. The means used to identify suffering of women in the military come from the advocacy surrounding female victims of sexual violence and also from veterans who served in Vietnam advocating for combat-related traumatic stress injuries. Therefore, a woman experiencing MST is somewhat positioned at the juncture of both of these movements. I believe it logically follows that she should be doubly visible. However, in reality, she usually ends up less visible.

"The ordinary response to atrocities is to banish them from consciousness."

Judith Herman, Trauma and Recovery

The Stat That Doesn't Add Up

Module 7 struck me particularly for a certain reason. In 2012, more active duty U.S. Army personnel committed suicide than were killed by enemy action in Afghanistan. I believe the exact figures were 177 and 176 respectively. I recall reading that and spending a few minutes at my kitchen table with my laptop open staring at that fact. There are certainly multiple ways to interpret that statistic. On one hand, war is awful. PTSD can kill people. Both are true. However, the lesser-known aspect of that statistic that bothers me more the more time I spend thinking about it is that a significant number of those suicides involved service members who had not been deployed to a combat zone at all. The narrative about PTSD related to combat that we have been sharing since Rambo does not address those individuals. Whatever is harming those individuals, whatever we do not understand well enough to name, is likely far greater than what we currently attribute to PTSD associated with combat.

The idea behind MST is correct; however, MST alone does not encompass everything. Deployment cycle wear and tear, the culture of hazing in many units, the fact that the majority of service members experiencing mental health issues typically have access to firearms within their homes are factors as well. However, MST is a large factor. This can be seen through the VA's own data which continually shows women who test positive for MST are more likely to receive a PTSD diagnosis than women testing negative. Likewise, this is true for men. The issue is that the nationwide discussion regarding military-related trauma rarely begins with MST. Rather it begins with an image of a helicopter. The discussion ends with a flag.

What the DSM Counts

The DSM uses a section titled Criterion A to define what constitutes "trauma" using the following criteria. Criterion A refers to events that occur "outside the range of usual human experience." Consider those words carefully. That is how the writers of this definition view everyday experiences. For many women, particularly those serving in the military, assault and harassment are within the range of typical experiences. They are part of it. DSM-5 revised some of this. Currently, the DSM-5 lists categories, actual or threatened death, serious injury, sexual violence, along with a separate provision for individuals such as first responders who may be exposed to traumatic details while performing their duties. MST falls under these definitions.

"Certain violations of the social compact are too terrible to utter aloud: this is the meaning of the word unspeakable."

Judith Herman, Trauma and Recovery

However, the gendered issue began earlier than Criterion A. The issue exists prior to Criterion A. The issue exists in the social narrative we provide to people describing how a traumatized person should appear. When the DSM category was finally created in 1980, the general population envisioned a trauma survivor as a man. More specifically, they envisioned him as a very lean young soldier returning from Vietnam who would not sleep. Herman has stated that both she and others working to document women's trauma contributed to the development of this clinical category, yet even she has acknowledged that the public perception of a trauma victim was based upon this archetype long before the clinical category developed.

Military Sexual Trauma, in Numbers

The term MST is the VA's acronym for sexual assault and severe sexual harassment experienced during active-duty time. It is screened for each veteran entering into any type of care provided by the VA. While the VA's numbers represent disclosures instead of incidents, they are the best available evidence for years now.

Approximately one-third of female veterans and approximately one in fifty male veterans disclose MST. As there are far more males serving in the military, although fewer are disclosing MST, there are still a considerable number of male victims. Additionally, research conducted by the VA in conjunction with Rachel Kimerling and her colleagues has demonstrated that MST has a stronger relationship to PTSD, depression and suicidal ideation among females than does combat-related PTSD.

I will mention another statistic from DoD reporting I continue to think about. Very few MST cases result in an unrestricted report; most remain restricted, meaning survivors receive treatment but no investigation occurs. Moreover, many MST cases go unreported altogether. It is understandable why this is occurring. Perpetrators often serve in the same small unit as the survivor and frequently in the same chain of command; retaliation against the survivor is a real concern; careers are extremely brief and easily terminated; all contribute to survivors remaining silent. Herman's point that denial is an ordinary response seems to me to be directly related to this context.

Why Men and Women Don't Get Diagnosed the Same Way

I am just beginning to learn some of this research as well. PTSD does not look the same across both genders. Men with PTSD typically will be identified by their clinicians through externalizing symptoms, anger, aggression, substance use, and most often others will recognize these behaviors as "bad" or unacceptable. Women are more likely to be identified through internalized symptoms, depression, anxiety, chronic pain, that may grind the person down but rarely alert anyone else. Some of this can be attributed to biological reasons; however, there are also significant sociological factors, including women being taught early in life to suppress emotions and express feelings internally. Lastly, there are certainly biases by clinicians due to mental templates of PTSD, as the mental image of PTSD commonly includes an angry and irritable male veteran, whereas a silently suffering female veteran is less recognizable. Studies that we reviewed in class demonstrated that women with PTSD were often diagnosed with depression first, sometimes for many years, before they ever received treatment related to their trauma.

For example, consider the case of a woman who serves in the military and experiences MST, military sexual trauma, and suffers PTSD symptoms until her late 20s. Finally, she enters a civilian clinic at age 30. She is more likely to leave the clinic with a prescription for Zoloft, an anti-depressant medication, and receive a generic diagnosis of anxiety than she is to be directed towards trauma-informed services. The diagnosis that was developed by Herman and her colleagues over several decades and placed within the DSM that corresponds with the type of injury this woman suffered is unlikely to be the one she receives upon leaving the clinic.

"Psychological trauma is an affliction of the powerless."

Judith Herman, Trauma and Recovery

Where Soldiers Come From

We viewed Heather Courtney's documentary Where Soldiers Come From during our class. This film documents the lives of three friends from Michigan's Upper Peninsula who enlisted in the National Guard together and deployed to Afghanistan. In the second half of the film there is a homecoming scene that I find myself replaying repeatedly. The three men come back home. They sit on the same couches. Their family owns the same dog. They still have the same wallpaper in their kitchen. The only thing that has changed since they left are the people inside themselves and those who surround them do not have words to describe the change.

One reason why I believe the documentary is so powerful in depicting men experiencing trauma is how silent the trauma is. The film contains no exploding bullets. There are no dramatic flashback scenes. Most of the time it is simply absence, sitting underneath the surface of everyday routines. As I sat viewing the film I kept thinking about how none of the women who experienced MST appear anywhere in the frame. If the small rural community of the Upper Peninsula cannot quite identify the trauma that occurred within three boys it sent to fight, what is expected of a young woman who comes back from a deployment where the only harm that occurred was not combat? What would be needed to tell a story similar to hers without having a boot planted firmly on ground and a blast of distant gunfire to cut away to?

What Courtney captured effectively for me was that re-integration does not occur instantly. Instead, it is a prolonged process of attempting to land. For a woman veteran with MST, this attempt is layered on top of a betrayal that is difficult for the non-veteran civilian society to understand or articulate. Herman describes three phases for healing, which include safety first, followed by reconstructing the narrative surrounding traumatic events, followed by reconnecting with community. Each phase presents unique barriers for MST survivors. Establishing safety is challenging if you have been harmed by an institution that now provides payment for your therapist. Reconstructing your experience is challenging when the official version of what transpired indicates that nothing occurred. Connecting with your community is challenging if they would prefer to remain ignorant of your trauma.

The Second Injury

The term "institutional betrayal," coined by psychologist Jennifer Freyd, describes the specific type of damage that occurs when an individual has placed trust within an institution for their well-being and the institution has responded to that person's victimization by protecting its own interests. Institutional betrayal in a military context takes many forms. Examples include the Climate Survey, administered to assess retaliation; the reporting system, the Chain of Command, that reports complaints to the alleged perpetrator's peers; administrative discharge occurring after a period of thirty days; and disappearance of documentation related to the original complaint.

Kirby Dick's 2012 documentary film, The Invisible War, exposed the scope of institutional betrayal in detail and prompted Congressional and Department of Defense action leading to some procedural reforms. While some of these reform actions were meaningful others were merely cosmetic. MST represents not only a traumatic event in terms of psychology, but also trauma whose process of healing is shaped and re-experienced through the very bureaucratic structure a survivor must navigate in order to obtain treatment. Herman wrote that a survivor's recovery from such an experience will be dependent upon a society that listens. Female veterans who suffer MST do not always live in a society that will listen. The VA has implemented MST Coordinators, which constitutes positive movement toward providing female veterans access to needed services, however, it is not a destination.

Who Gets to Be a Veteran

In addition to bureaucratic layers of the problem there are also cultural layers. Historically, women serving in the U.S. military have been socially constructed as being peripheral to the "true" military. Nurses. Admin personnel. Logistics clerks. Supporting roles. Even if the role a woman veteran served in included putting herself in harms way during convoys or while flying helicopters, the societal stereotype continues to be embedded. Once a female veteran returns home with PTSD symptoms she must accomplish something that her male counterpart did not have to accomplish. She must prove that she is indeed a veteran and that her experiences were part of a legitimate deployment. Additionally, she must prove that the trauma she experienced as a result of MST should receive equal consideration to the trauma a civilian pictures when they picture war.

This can be seen demonstrated clearly in television shows such as Homeland. Intelligence work performed by Carrie Mathison puts her under extreme stress and the series regularly portrays her mental deterioration as a result of her feminine frailty versus an occupational hazard. Similar examples of this phenomenon exist in media references beyond the series. Many journalistic articles about female veterans suffering from MST utilize the term "survivor" to refer to women veterans instead of using the term "veteran," even though men would never be referred to by such terminology. These are subtle differences, yet they represent how a society communicates its beliefs regarding categories.

"It is very tempting to take the side of the perpetrator. All the perpetrator asks is that the bystander do nothing."

Judith Herman, Trauma and Recovery

What Counts as Coming Home

When I began my essay, I used statistics; therefore, I wanted to finish with the question these same statistics were really asking. If you agree that traumatic stress is a very real phenomenon, that the military can be a workplace for many service members, and that most service members have never experienced the Hollywood version of combat that we generally see and hear about as the normative military experience, then your definition of what constitutes "military" trauma determines who receives treatment.

The expansion of categories of traumatic experiences has been influenced by the demand for recognition by survivors and their advocacy organizations. Herman noted that the category of PTSD expanded because of the advocacy efforts of Vietnam veterans, Holocaust survivors, and feminist researchers. Perhaps it will be necessary for women veterans who have experienced MST to advocate again, but this time they should insist that MST be recognized as a combat injury and not simply as a footnote to the typical male military experience. The quote from Herman that I continually find myself returning to is her statement regarding how atrocity often elicits an ordinary response of denial. Denial is ordinary because it is less costly than acknowledging reality. Acknowledging reality is more expensive than ignoring it.

Men in my family have served in the military since before I was born. I do not intend to diminish or devalue their military experiences. Instead, I would like to reject the stereotypical image of the soldier which excludes the women who served alongside them. These women suffered injuries which cannot be described using a helicopter and a rifle. My primary takeaway from this class is that the type of traumatic event that we choose to deny does not simply cease to exist. Rather, those types of events will continue to be denied and subsequently untreated. Naming the trauma is merely the first step toward addressing the trauma.
